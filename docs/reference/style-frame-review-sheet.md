# Style Frame Review Sheet

Use this sheet to review a still frame, storyboard panel, or animatic shot before it moves into render.

The goal is not to imitate any one studio. The goal is to achieve:

- immediate readability for a child audience
- clean emotional clarity
- scientifically correct visual logic
- a warm, premium, storybook feel

The frame also has to feel believable in context: the scene should match the described beat, the visible action should make sense on its own, the background should belong to the subject, and the palette of any cards, labels, or placeholder shapes should belong to the scene rather than compete with it.

Use this after a frame is drafted and before production time is spent on refinement.

---

## Review Order

Review in this order:

1. `1-second glance`
2. `5-second comprehension`
3. `silent-frame check`
4. `subtitle-safe check`
5. `science-accuracy check`
6. `motion-readiness check`
7. `palette-fit check`
8. `equation-render check`

If a frame fails an early check, stop and fix that before scoring the rest.

---

## Pass / Revise Rule

Mark each item as:

- `Pass`
- `Revise`
- `Fail`

Decision rule:

- `Pass` if all core checks pass and there are no factual or readability problems
- `Revise` if the frame is directionally right but needs cleanup
- `Fail` if the frame is confusing, inaccurate, overcrowded, or not readable at a glance

Any `Fail` in science accuracy, subtitle safety, or focal clarity blocks the frame.
Any `Fail` in palette fit or equation rendering also blocks the frame, because those failures usually require redesign rather than polish.

## Core Checks

### 1. One-Glance Readability

- Can I tell what the shot is about in one second?
- Is there one clear focal point?
- Does the silhouette read cleanly at thumbnail size?
- Is the visual hierarchy obvious without narration?

Red flags:

- too many equally important objects
- no clear center of attention
- details only become visible after zooming in

### 2. Five-Second Comprehension

- Can I explain the shot in one short sentence after five seconds?
- Does the frame communicate the intended beat, not just a pretty composition?
- Is the scene type obvious: character, object, equation, comparison, timeline, motion?
- Does the visible action match the narrated beat, instead of relying on implied context?

Red flags:

- the image looks nice but the beat is vague
- viewers would need the voiceover to know what they are seeing

### 3. Science Accuracy

- Are the objects, labels, and directions correct?
- Are signs, arrows, and relationships physically or mathematically valid?
- Does the frame avoid a false shortcut that changes the meaning?
- If this is a simplification, is the simplification obvious and safe?
- Does the scene feel physically believable, including where characters stand and how solid they look?

Red flags:

- impossible geometry used for convenience
- mixed historical eras
- arrows or labels that imply the wrong cause
- equation symbols moved for decoration
- a naturally moving object is shown static when the beat depends on its motion

### 4. Storybook Fit

- Does the frame match the board-book visual language?
- Are the colors warm, flat, and readable?
- Are the characters or objects rounded, friendly, and simple?
- Does the page feel premium rather than generic?
- Do any text cards, ribbons, or shapes use colors that fit the current scene palette?

Red flags:

- glossy 3D rendering
- harsh neon color
- sterile corporate illustration
- too much visual noise
- decorative text boxes that are larger than the text they support

### 5. Caption Safety

- Is the subtitle reserve kept clear?
- Are key labels out of the lower third?
- Will captions collide with the focal object or equation?
- Does the frame still work if the platform overlays text?

Red flags:

- important content in the subtitle zone
- lower-third text crowding
- title card too low in frame

### 6. Motion Readiness

- Can this shot animate clearly with one simple motion beat?
- Is the intended motion obvious from the composition?
- Does the frame support a clean entrance, reveal, hold, and exit?
- Will the motion help explanation instead of distracting from it?
- If the focal object naturally moves, is the planned motion physically meaningful rather than a static hold?

### 7. Palette Fit

- Do the added cards, labels, and accent shapes belong to the palette already established by the scene?
- Are the bright accents intentional, or do they clash with the background and break the mood?
- Would this still look coherent if the same colors were used in a full scene sequence?

Red flags:

- generic red, blue, or purple blocks dropped into a warm scene without reason
- placeholder shapes that look like default UI instead of storybook design
- cards that overpower the scene instead of supporting it

### 8. Equation Render

- Do equations render as real math, including fractions, integrals, superscripts, subscripts, and symbols?
- Are all indices legible at the intended size?
- Does the equation remain readable without flattening it into plain text?
- If the equation is long, is it broken into a readable sequence rather than crammed into one line?

Red flags:

- math pasted as unformatted text
- superscripts or subscripts lost in rendering
- symbols too small to read in the frame

Red flags:

- the frame only works when frozen
- animation would need too many moving parts
- the motion would be decorative rather than explanatory
- pendulums, springs, or similar objects are shown as still images when the teaching beat depends on their motion

## Clarity Tests

Apply these before approving a shot.

### The Thumbnail Test

Shrink the frame to phone size.

Ask:

- can I still identify the subject
- can I still identify the scene type
- does the focal point survive the shrink

### The Mute Test

Look at the frame without audio.

Ask:

- does the shot still communicate the right beat
- does the visual order make sense on its own

### The Freeze Test

Pause the animatic on this shot.

Ask:

- is the frame compositionally balanced
- does the pause feel intentional
- does anything read as accidental clutter

### The Caption Test

Overlay the expected subtitle block.

Ask:

- do any labels collide with captions
- does the lower third remain usable
- if the text is long, should it be rolled or staged over time inside the same card instead of shown all at once
- if unsure, follow the rolling-text policy in `powerpoint_layout_rules.md`

### The Preview Audio Test

Check the preview asset itself, not just the scene.

Ask:

- is audio present and intentional, or is the preview silent by design
- if audio is absent, is that explicitly noted in the preview output or filename
- would a reviewer be able to tell whether a missing voice track is expected or a bug

### The Scientist Test

Explain the frame in one sentence using correct physics.

If that sentence requires hand-waving, the frame needs revision.

## Rating Scale

Score each area from `0` to `2`.

- `2` = strong and ready
- `1` = acceptable but needs cleanup
- `0` = broken or misleading

Categories:

- focal clarity
- composition
- caption safety
- scientific accuracy
- emotional warmth
- style consistency
- motion readability

Suggested threshold:

- `12+` total and no zeroes = approve
- `9-11` = revise
- `8 or less` = reject

Do not override a zero in scientific accuracy.

---

## Common Failure Patterns

- the frame tries to show too many ideas at once
- a correction or label is too small to read
- the composition is beautiful but the science is unclear
- the motion plan is more important than the still frame
- the visual style is cute but the explanation is too vague
- the frame looks polished but would fail on a vertical crop
- the card colors clash with the scene palette
- the character or object appears to float, vanish, or turn transparent in a way that breaks realism
- the math is technically present but not actually rendered as readable notation

---

## Recommended Review Comments

Use concrete comments, not vague taste notes.

Good comments:

- `Move the equation up to clear the caption zone.`
- `Reduce the scene to one focal object.`
- `The arrow implies the wrong direction of force.`
- `This needs a stronger silhouette at thumbnail size.`
- `The historical costume is mixing eras.`

Avoid:

- `Make it nicer.`
- `Feels off.`
- `Too busy.`

---

## Approval Checklist

Approve only if all are true:

- the frame reads instantly
- the intended beat is obvious
- the science is correct
- the subtitle reserve is clean
- the visual language matches the storybook style guide
- the shot can be animated without redesigning the composition

If any one of these fails, send the shot back for revision before rendering.
