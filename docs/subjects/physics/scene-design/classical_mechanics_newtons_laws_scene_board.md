## Board Rules

- Visual language: board-book inspired, warm pastel, flat shapes, rounded characters, friendly outlines.
- Layout language: one primary idea per scene, captions safe in the lower third, avoid clutter.
- Accuracy rule: do not simplify away the graph logic that produces `d = 1/2at^2`.
- Motion rule: prefer tactile, readable motion over cinematic flourish.

---

## Scene 1

- Narration beat: Galileo, a ramp, some brass balls, and a stubborn measurement habit.
- Scene type: `character_intro` + `historical_setting`
- Layout notes: Galileo sits slightly left of center in a Pisa laboratory; ramp and water clock occupy the right side; title card/ribbon at the top; background in warm cream or butter yellow with a paper-texture look.
- Assets: Galileo character, wooden inclined ramp, brass balls, water clock, simple lab props, title ribbon.
- Motion: gentle entrance of the character, slight pop-in of the ramp, tiny bounce on the title ribbon.
- Accuracy notes: keep the time period explicit; do not show modern lab gear; the ramp should look handmade and simple.

## Scene 2

- Narration beat: Aristotle's claim that heavier objects fall faster, illustrated with the stone-and-feather contrast.
- Scene type: `comparison_split`
- Layout notes: split frame with stone on one side and feather on the other; keep the comparison clean and symbolic, not photoreal.
- Assets: stone, feather, subtle motion arrows, short caption tags.
- Motion: stone drops quickly, feather drifts more slowly; use a single timed beat to show the perceived difference.
- Accuracy notes: this is the historically common intuition, not a modern physics claim; avoid implying Galileo used a vacuum chamber.

## Scene 3

- Narration beat: the logical contradiction in Aristotle's theory when heavy and light stones are tied together.
- Scene type: `diagram_explain`
- Layout notes: use a simple thought-experiment diagram in the main zone; place the two stones and the tied pair as separate visual states in sequence.
- Assets: two stone icons, rope connection, arrows showing fast/slow, contradiction marker.
- Motion: the two separate stones fade into a tied pair, then a visual question mark or tension mark appears.
- Accuracy notes: preserve the logical structure of the argument; do not oversell it as a direct experimental proof.

## Scene 4

- Narration beat: Galileo slows motion by tilting the path into a shallow ramp.
- Scene type: `motion_animation`
- Layout notes: a single long ramp spans the frame diagonally; Galileo can be small in the corner pointing at it; keep the motion path obvious.
- Assets: shallow ramp, brass ball, Galileo pointer pose, simple angle marker.
- Motion: the ball rolls downward slowly with a visible path trace.
- Accuracy notes: the ramp should be a pedagogical stand-in for slow acceleration; do not add unnecessary decorative objects that distract from the ramp geometry.

## Scene 5

- Narration beat: the water clock method and the idea of measuring elapsed time by weight of water.
- Scene type: `object_demo`
- Layout notes: water clock on one side, collection vessel or measured droplets on the other; keep the lower third free for subtitles.
- Assets: water clock, droplet stream, weighing pan, simple measurement marks.
- Motion: droplets fall steadily; the weight container fills in small increments.
- Accuracy notes: keep the water clock explanation literal and modest; avoid making it look like a modern stopwatch.

## Scene 6

- Narration beat: the data table showing 1, 4, 9, 16 and the discovery that distance scales with time squared.
- Scene type: `graph_plot` + `data_table`
- Layout notes: table on the left or center with large readable numerals; the right side can hold a simple visual cue that the values are squares.
- Assets: clean number table, small square icons or grid marks, emphasis highlight on each row.
- Motion: rows appear one at a time, then the square pattern is highlighted across the column.
- Accuracy notes: do not collapse the observation into a vague "it gets bigger faster"; the square pattern is the point.

## Scene 7

- Narration beat: the equation `d proportional to t^2`.
- Scene type: `equation_reveal`
- Layout notes: centered equation in a framed card; use a warm cream background with coral or teal accents; leave space for symbol labels.
- Assets: equation card, small annotation bubbles, light geometric border.
- Motion: equation fades in, then the `t^2` term pulses once.
- Accuracy notes: this is proportionality, not the final full equation yet; do not present it as `d = 1/2at^2` prematurely.

## Scene 8

- Narration beat: velocity-time graph and the meaning of slope.
- Scene type: `graph_plot`
- Layout notes: graph occupies the center with labeled axes; keep labels large and readable; use a clean monotone background so the graph is the focus.
- Assets: `v` vs `t` graph, slope marker, axis labels, colored line.
- Motion: line draws from origin upward; a small marker travels along the line.
- Accuracy notes: the slope must be labeled as acceleration; do not invert the axes or imply that area is speed.

## Scene 9

- Narration beat: the triangle under the velocity-time graph and the area argument for distance.
- Scene type: `equation_reveal` + `diagram_explain`
- Layout notes: reuse the graph, but now shade the triangle underneath; label base and height directly on the visual.
- Assets: graph, shaded triangle, base label `t`, height label `at`, area formula badge.
- Motion: the shaded region fills in after the graph line appears; labels pop onto base and height in sequence.
- Accuracy notes: the area interpretation must remain explicit; this is the derivation step that makes the one-half inevitable.

## Scene 10

- Narration beat: the full equation `d = 1/2at^2` and a worked numerical example on a ramp.
- Scene type: `equation_reveal` + `worked_example`
- Layout notes: split layout with the final equation on one side and a small numerical ramp example on the other; keep the final equation large and dominant.
- Assets: final equation card, ramp with numeric tags, distance markers, velocity markers.
- Motion: equation locks in first, then the numbers populate the worked example step by step.
- Accuracy notes: do not hide the meaning of each symbol; keep units visible; preserve the difference between proportionality and equality.

## Scene 11

- Narration beat: the double-ramp thought experiment that shows why motion persists.
- Scene type: `motion_animation`
- Layout notes: use a left ramp, flat middle, and right ramp that progressively flattens; the ball should be the single moving focus.
- Assets: double-ramp setup, ball, height guide, several ramp variants.
- Motion: the ball rolls down, rises on the second ramp, and the second ramp is shown progressively shallower until flat.
- Accuracy notes: frame this as a thought experiment and idealization; do not imply it was a frictionless real-world measurement.

## Scene 12

- Narration beat: the conclusion that motion continues naturally and the bridge to Newton's First Law.
- Scene type: `comparison_split` + `outro_bridge`
- Layout notes: left side can show a ball rolling on a flat surface toward the horizon; right side can show a short text card with the episode takeaway and the next-episode hook.
- Assets: rolling ball, infinite flat surface illusion, short law summary card, next-episode ribbon.
- Motion: the ball continues with a steady glide; the takeaway card slides in at the end.
- Accuracy notes: keep the statement careful: Galileo establishes the inertial idea, Newton formalizes it later as the First Law.
